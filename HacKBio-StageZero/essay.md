**Genomic Bias: When AI Learns from Unequal DNA Data**



**Introduction**

Artificial intelligence is rapidly becoming a powerful tool for reading our genomes and helping doctors make decisions. But these models are only as fair as the data they learn from. Today, most genomic datasets used to train AI systems overrepresent people of European ancestry and underrepresent populations from Africa and other regions \[1, 2]. This imbalance raises a critical question: when AI learns from unequal data, who benefits and who gets left behind?

**Main body**

Genomic databases are the foundation for AI tools that predict disease risk, guide drug development, and support clinical decision making. Yet global studies show that individuals of African ancestry, despite having the greatest genetic diversity, make up only a small fraction of genomes in major research datasets \[1, 3]. As a result, algorithms trained mostly on European genomes often perform poorly when applied to African or other underrepresented populations.

This problem has real consequences for health equity. AI driven genetic risk scores and diagnostic tools may underestimate disease risk or miss important variants in groups that were barely included in training data. Studies of medical AI have already documented cases where models are less accurate for minority populations because the systems effectively “learn” the biases and gaps present in the data. In genomics, this could mean late diagnosis, inappropriate treatment choices, or exclusion from the benefits of precision medicine \[4].

At the same time, underrepresentation limits scientific discovery itself \[5]. African genomes, for example, contain rich genetic diversity that can reveal new disease mechanisms and therapeutic targets. When these populations are missing from datasets, AI models may fail to detect variants that are important for malaria, sickle cell disease, or other conditions that disproportionately affect low  and middle income countries. In this way, data gaps in genomics can reinforce existing global health inequalities instead of helping to close them.

Addressing genomic bias requires more than simply collecting more data. Researchers and policymakers are calling for deliberate efforts to build diverse biobanks, invest in sequencing infrastructure in underrepresented regions, and design AI methods that explicitly monitor and correct for bias \[6, 7]. Ethicists also stress the importance of community engagement, fair benefit sharing and governance frameworks that prevent exploitative data practices. Together, these efforts aim to ensure that genomic AI systems are powerful and fair.

**Conclusion**

AI in genomics holds enormous promise, but biased data can turn a powerful tool into a driver of new inequities. If most of the world’s genomes in training datasets come from a narrow slice of humanity, AI will inevitably work best for that slice. Building fair and trustworthy genomic AI means ensuring that the diversity of human populations is reflected in the data and that the benefits of these technologies are shared globally, not just by a privileged few.
